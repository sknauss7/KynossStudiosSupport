Dealer Doc Fees by State (2026): Caps, Typical Charges, and When to Push Back

12 states cap the documentation fee by law. The other 38 charge whatever you'll sign. Here's every state's number.

Benchmarks as of 2026-04-23 · All 50 states · Sources cited below

The documentation fee — "doc fee," "dealer processing fee," "admin fee" — is what the dealer charges to prepare your paperwork. In most states it's dealer profit, not a government charge, and it ranges from $85 (capped, California) to $999+ (Florida, uncapped) for the exact same paperwork.

Are doc fees negotiable? The fee itself rarely comes off — but the deal is negotiable. If the doc fee is high for your state, ask for the vehicle price or your trade-in value to move by the same amount. In capped states, anything above the cap isn't a negotiation — it's an error to be corrected.

APR markup: the fee that isn't on any line

Doc fees are visible. The bigger cost usually isn't: when the dealer arranges your financing, the lender approves you at a buy rate — and the dealer is allowed to write the contract at a higher rate and keep a share of the difference. Two extra points on a $30,000, 72-month loan is roughly $2,000 over the life of the loan, and it never appears as a line item.

The defense is knowing what rate your credit actually earns. These are the average new- and used-car APRs by credit tier (Experian, Q1 2025):

Credit tier New Used
Super Prime (781+) 5.18% 6.82%
Prime (661-780) 6.70% 9.06%
Near Prime (601-660) 9.83% 13.74%
Subprime (501-600) 13.22% 18.99%
Deep Subprime (300-500) 15.81% 21.58%

Averages assume a 60-month term; expect roughly +0.3% per extra 12 months of term. Your quoted rate also moves with down payment, vehicle age, and lender.

  • Get pre-approved first. A bank or credit union pre-approval is your real benchmark — and your walk-away power.
  • Ask the F&I manager directly: "What's the buy rate?" They don't have to answer, but the question signals you know the mechanism.
  • Compare the contract APR to your tier's average above. More than a point or two over — with good credit — means the rate deserves an explanation.

Doc fee questions, answered

Are dealer doc fees negotiable?

The fee itself usually isn't — most dealers treat it as fixed and many states require charging it uniformly to every buyer. What is negotiable is the bottom line: if the doc fee is high for your state, ask for the vehicle price or trade-in value to move by the same amount. In the 12 states with a legal cap, anything above the cap is simply not permitted — ask for it to be corrected, not negotiated.

What is a dealer doc fee?

A documentation (or 'doc') fee is what the dealer charges to prepare and file your paperwork — title, registration, contract processing. It is dealer profit in most states, not a government charge, and it varies from $85 to over $1,000 depending on where you buy.

Which states cap doc fees in 2026?

12 states cap the dealer doc fee by statute or regulation: California ($85), Minnesota ($125), Oregon ($150), New York ($175), Louisiana ($200), Ohio ($250), Michigan ($260), Illinois ($324.24), Maryland ($500), Missouri ($500), Colorado ($699), Virginia ($899). The other 38 states have no hard cap.

Can a dealer charge more than the state cap?

No. In capped states the maximum is set by statute or regulation. If the doc fee on your buyer's order exceeds your state's cap, point at the line and ask for it to be corrected before you sign.

What is APR markup (dealer reserve)?

When a dealer arranges your loan, the lender approves you at a 'buy rate.' The dealer is allowed to present you a higher rate and keep a share of the difference as compensation — typically up to 2 percentage points. It never appears as a line item. The defense: get pre-approved by your own bank or credit union first, then ask the dealer to beat that rate.
